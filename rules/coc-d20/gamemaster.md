# Chapter 10: The Gamemaster

This chapter provides guidance for Gamemasters running Call of Cthulhu d20 campaigns. It covers storytelling techniques, atmosphere creation, rules adjudication, and campaign management.

## Storytelling

Call of Cthulhu is fundamentally about telling horror stories collaboratively. The GM is the primary storyteller, but players contribute significantly to the narrative.

### Creating Compelling Stories

#### Mystery and Discovery

Good Call of Cthulhu adventures follow a pattern:

1. **Hook**: Investigators receive a call to adventure
2. **Investigation**: Clues lead deeper into mystery
3. **Escalation**: Danger increases as truth emerges
4. **Climax**: Confrontation with Mythos horror
5. **Resolution**: Survival at a cost

#### Pacing

- Start slow, building atmosphere and tension
- Reveal information gradually through investigation
- Build to moments of genuine terror
- Allow breathing room between intense scenes
- End on a note that leaves players unsettled

### GM Advice: Save or Check?

When determining if a character notices something or succeeds at an action, decide whether to call for a skill check or simply tell the player:

#### Call for a Check When:
- Success is uncertain and meaningful
- Failure has interesting consequences
- The character has relevant skills
- Tension would increase from uncertainty

#### Don't Call for a Check When:
- The character must succeed to continue (provide clues another way)
- Automatic success or failure is more dramatic
- No character has relevant skills (GM narration)
- Rolling would slow the game unnecessarily

## Atmosphere

Atmosphere is crucial in horror gaming. Create it through:

### Descriptive Language

Use sensory details to immerse players:

**Visual**: Dim light, shadows that move, unnatural geometry  
**Auditory**: Distant whispers, scratching in walls, silence too complete  
**Olfactory**: Rot, ozone, sulfur, metallic tang of blood  
**Tactile**: Cold spots, slimy surfaces, vibrations  

### Pacing and Timing

- **Slow builds**: Long descriptions create tension
- **Sudden shocks**: Brief, punchy sentences for scares
- **Silence**: Pauses before revelations build anticipation
- **Revelation**: Save key information for dramatic moments

### Setting the Scene

#### Lighting and Environment

If playing in person:
- Dim lights during tense scenes
- Use candles or lanterns for period atmosphere
- Close curtains to block outside light

#### Sound Effects

- Background music (ambient, not distracting)
- Sound effects for specific events
- Silence can be more effective than noise

## Fear, Shock, and Horror

Different types of fear affect players differently:

### Fear Types

| Type          | Description                                    | Example                          |
|---------------|------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| Dread         | Anticipation of something terrible             | Hearing footsteps approaching    |
| Surprise      | Sudden shock or jump scare                     | Monster leaps from closet        |
| Revulsion     | Disgust at grotesque imagery                   | Mutilated corpse, writhing worms |
| Existential   | Horror at cosmic insignificance                | Realizing humanity is meaningless|

### Building Dread

Dread is more effective than shock:

1. **Foreshadowing**: Hints of coming horror
2. **Uncertainty**: Players don't know what's coming
3. **Escalation**: Each clue reveals worse truth
4. **Helplessness**: Characters cannot fight everything

### Avoiding Desensitization

- Don't overuse shocking imagery
- Vary the types of horror presented
- Allow players moments of safety
- Respect player boundaries

## The Supernatural

The supernatural should feel alien and wrong:

### Making Horror Alien

- **Non-Euclidean geometry**: Spaces that don't make sense
- **Unnatural colors**: Shades beyond normal spectrum
- **Impossible sounds**: Frequencies that hurt to hear
- **Wrong physics**: Objects moving against gravity

### Sanity and Horror

Sanity loss should feel meaningful:

#### When to Call for Loss

- First encounter with Mythos entity
- Witnessing gruesome death or mutilation
- Reading forbidden texts
- Experiencing reality distortion

#### Varying the Impact

- Personal connection increases loss
- Helplessness increases loss
- Prolonged exposure increases loss
- Repeated similar encounters decrease loss (getting used to it)

## Refereeing the Game

The GM interprets and enforces rules. Balance consistency with dramatic needs.

### Using the Rules

#### When to Enforce Strictly

- Combat mechanics during fights
- Sanity loss from defined triggers
- Spell costs and requirements
- Equipment and resource management

#### When to Be Flexible

- Skill checks that would stall progress
- Edge cases not covered by rules
- Player creative solutions
- Dramatic moments requiring rule bending

### Adjudicating Disputes

1. **Listen**: Hear all sides of the argument
2. **Rule Quickly**: Make a decision and move on
3. **Note for Later**: Look up exact ruling after scene
4. **Be Consistent**: Apply similar rulings similarly

## NPC Attitudes

Non-player characters have attitudes toward investigators:

### Attitude Scale

| Attitude    | Behavior                                    | Interaction DC   |
|-------------|---------------------------------------------|------------------|
| Hostile     | Actively antagonistic, may attack           | +20 or higher    |
| Unfriendly  | Dislikes investigators, uncooperative       | +15              |
| Indifferent | Doesn't care either way                     | +10 (baseline)   |
| Friendly    | Likes investigators, willing to help        | +5               |
| Helpful     | Actively supportive, goes out of way        | 0 or automatic   |

### Changing Attitudes

**Diplomacy checks** can shift attitudes:
- Successful check moves attitude one step better
- Failed check may worsen attitude
- Time and actions also affect attitudes

#### Factors Affecting Attitude

| Factor                  | Effect on Attitude               |
|-------------------------|----------------------------------|
| Shared goals            | +1 or +2 steps                   |
| Common enemies          | +1 step                          |
| Investigators' reputation| Varies widely                    |
| Bribes and gifts        | +1 step (temporary)              |
| Threats                 | -1 to -2 steps                   |
| Past betrayals          | -2 or more steps                 |

## Experience and Improvement

Characters improve through play. Balance advancement with the horror theme.

### Experience Points

Award XP for:
- **Overcoming challenges**: 50-100 XP per encounter
- **Roleplaying**: 25-50 XP for good character portrayal
- **Story milestones**: 100-200 XP at adventure conclusions
- **Sanity loss**: 10 XP per point lost (encourages horror)

### Level Progression

| Level | Total XP   | Benefits                              |
|-------|------------|---------------------------------------|
| 1st   | 0          | Starting character                    |
| 2nd   | 1,000      | New skills, feats                     |
| 3rd   | 3,000      | Feat, improved saves                  |
| 4th   | 6,000      | Ability score increase                |
| 5th   | 10,000     | More skill ranks                      |
| 6th   | 15,000     | Feat, multiple attacks possible       |

### Improvement Guidelines

#### Don't Let Characters Become Too Powerful

- Mythos entities should remain threatening
- Higher levels mean more Sanity at risk
- Consider capping levels for horror campaigns (8-12 recommended)

#### Sanity and Advancement

- Maximum Sanity can increase with Wisdom
- But Cthulhu Mythos skill reduces maximum Sanity
- Balance knowledge gains against mental stability

## Campaign Management

### Session Structure

**Typical 4-hour session:**
- 30 min: Recap previous session
- 90 min: Investigation and roleplay
- 60 min: Climax and resolution
- 30 min: Aftermath and setup for next session

### Between Sessions

- Send recap emails to players
- Prepare notes for upcoming scenes
- Track NPC relationships and clues
- Update character sheets as needed

### Long-Term Campaigns

#### Character Death

Death is common in Call of Cthulhu. Plan for it:
- Encourage players to have backup characters
- Allow new investigators to join existing groups
- Consider legacy mechanics (characters related to previous ones)

#### Story Arcs

Plan multi-session arcs:
- **One-shot**: Single session mystery
- **Short arc**: 3-5 sessions, one location/entity
- **Long campaign**: 10+ sessions, multiple connected mysteries

### Tracking Information

Keep organized records of:
- Clues given and received
- NPC attitudes and relationships
- Player choices and consequences
- Sanity loss and recovery
- Items acquired and used
